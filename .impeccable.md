## Design Context

### Users
LintasNusa serves Indonesian businesses that need logistics to feel measurable instead of fragmented. The primary audiences are D2C brands, UMKM and marketplace sellers, mid-market distributors, and temperature-sensitive operators evaluating or using a digital-first logistics partner. Secondary audiences include recipient-side users who need tracking clarity, rescheduling, and safe-drop control, plus enterprise stakeholders in operations, finance, and customer service who need one shared system.

The core jobs to be done are:
- understand whether LintasNusa is a credible logistics partner,
- compare services and operational proof quickly,
- access one ecosystem for shipping, fulfillment, returns, claims, and reporting,
- reduce uncertainty through visibility, accountability, and clear recovery flows.

Users are high-intent and task-focused. They are not looking for promo energy. They need fast comprehension, trust signals, measurable service language, and interfaces that feel operationally disciplined on both desktop and mobile.

### Brand Personality
The brand should feel **lucid, dependable, and composed**.

Its voice is:
- clear, not theatrical,
- confident, not loud,
- specific, not generic,
- operational, not bureaucratic,
- human, but not slang-heavy.

The emotional target is not excitement for its own sake. The interface should create confidence, control, and calm accountability. LintasNusa should feel like the most coherent logistics option in the room: modern, credible, premium, and distinctly Indonesian without slipping into folklore or campaign-style noise.

### Aesthetic Direction
The visual direction is **logistics infrastructure meets modern SaaS**.

This should translate into:
- a light-first, trust-forward presentation for public-facing brand and company-profile surfaces,
- structured layouts with strong grids, generous whitespace, and fewer but stronger claims,
- visual cues drawn from cross-island movement, route systems, map geometry, control towers, and dashboard overlays,
- imagery that prioritizes seller workflows, recipient moments, warehousing, and operational proof over generic truck-and-box clichés.

The palette direction from project context is a navy-led system with teal and signal-orange accents, supported by misted neutrals and charcoal text. Accent use should stay disciplined and proof-oriented, not decorative or promo-heavy.

The product should explicitly avoid:
- generic courier-brochure styling,
- discount-chaotic ecommerce motifs,
- noisy montages and handshake stock imagery,
- empty adjectives without measurable proof,
- hard-sell CTAs that make the brand feel like a campaign landing page.

### Design Principles
1. **Proof before slogans.** Every quality claim should be backed by numbers, mechanism, or visible evidence.
2. **Clarity is the product.** Interfaces should reduce ambiguity and make flows, states, and responsibilities legible at a glance.
3. **One ecosystem, many roles.** Sender, recipient, merchant, finance, and operations experiences should feel connected rather than split into unrelated surfaces.
4. **Premium through discipline.** Use restraint, precision, and composure instead of hype, clutter, or visual overstatement.
5. **Indonesian by context, not costume.** Ground the work in real regional commerce, language, and movement patterns without relying on folkloric decoration.
6. **Consultative over transactional.** The brand should guide operational decisions and partnerships, not push impulse conversion patterns.

### Open Assumptions
- No explicit accessibility target is defined in the current project context, so future work should default to strong semantic structure, keyboard access, high contrast, and reduced-motion support.
- No formal reference or anti-reference product list is documented yet beyond the stated desire to avoid generic courier and promo-heavy aesthetics.
- Public-facing surfaces should default to light mode unless a specific product surface has a stronger operational reason to go dark.
